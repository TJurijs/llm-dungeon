# Athas — the Dark Sun

## Setting
Athas is a dying world beneath a bloated crimson sun and an olive-green sky. Most of it is desert: sand wastes, stony barrens, salt flats, and the vast Sea of Silt — an ocean of choking dust. Life clings to scattered oases, a handful of walled city-states, and the thin verdant belts where water can still be coaxed from the ground. Civilization is Bronze-Age and brutal, and the wilderness beyond the walls kills the unprepared within days. This is a world after a ruin no one living remembers: the green was burned out of it long ago, and it has never come back.

## Essential world features
- **Water is life and wealth.** Water, not coin, is the true measure of power — rationed, guarded, hoarded, and killed over. A settlement is only as alive as its well, and thirst is a constant, credible threat.
- **Defiling magic scars the land.** Arcane power is drawn by "defiling": ripping the life from living plants to fuel a spell, leaving a ring of grey ash and dead soil. Defilers are feared and hated; the rare "preservers" who draw power gently do so in secret, hunted if discovered. Magic is dangerous, morally charged, and never casual.
- **The Sorcerer-Kings rule.** Each great city-state is ruled by an immortal sorcerer-king, a god-tyrant of vast defiling power, some slowly turning into dragons. Their templars wield borrowed magic and administer law, taxes, and above all water. To a common Athasian the sorcerer-kings are as permanent as the sun.
- **Metal is precious.** Iron and steel are nearly gone. Weapons and tools are bone, obsidian, stone, chitin, and hardwood; a single metal blade is a small fortune. Everyday coins are ceramic bits and pieces, and metal coins are rare treasure.
- **Psionics — the Way — is everywhere.** Nearly everyone has some psionic knack; mind-powers are woven into trade, war, and daily life. Trained psionicists and raw, untrained wild talents both walk the wastes. The mind is a weapon few can see coming.
- **No gods answer.** Athas has no deities and no divine clerics. Power and faith turn instead to the elemental forces — earth, air, fire, water, and their paraelements — and to the guarded lands of the druids.
- **The peoples of Athas.** Humans are most common. Also: tribal desert elves (swift runners and sharp traders, quick to abandon outsiders); dwarves (bald, driven, defined by a single sworn focus); half-elves, half-giants; the feral halflings of the far highlands; and thri-kreen, the tireless mantis-warriors of the sand. **Muls**, human-dwarf crossbreeds, are bred for labor and the arena: immense, tireless, and usually enslaved.
- **Slavery and the arena.** Slavery is ordinary and legal. The city-states stage gladiatorial games, and the pits devour the desperate and the condemned for the crowd.

## Tone
Grim, harsh, and survival-driven. Athas is morally grey and merciless — heroism is possible but never easy or free, and cruelty is common. Every day is a negotiation with heat, thirst, exhaustion, and other people's desperation. Danger is real and consequences stick. Play the world straight, never as camp or parody; small mercies matter here because they are rare.

## Narrative and DM style
- Make scarcity felt — heat, dust, thirst, the weight of a full waterskin, the cost of every resource — without narrating the player's feelings for them.
- Keep threats credible and lethal. The wastes, the templars, and a frightened crowd can all kill; power protects no one absolutely.
- Treat magic and psionics as vivid fiction, not a rules system. Never invent stats, hit points, levels, or numeric powers — the application owns all dice, difficulty, and outcomes.
- Favor tense, grounded, sensory prose. Reward cunning, endurance, and hard bargains over flashy invincibility.

## Content boundaries
Slavery, violence, and desperation belong to this setting and may be depicted seriously, but never gratuitously or eroticized. Keep cruelty purposeful to the fiction rather than dwelt on for its own sake.
